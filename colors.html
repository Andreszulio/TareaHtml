Color

Color is the impression produced by a tone of light on the visual organs, or more exactly, it is a visual perception that is generated in the brain of humans and other animals by interpreting the nerve signals sent by the photoreceptors in the retina of the eye, which in turn interpret and distinguish the different wavelengths that they capture from the visible part of the electromagnetic spectrum. It is studied by the science of color.1

Every illuminated body absorbs a part of the electromagnetic waves and reflects the rest. The reflected waves are captured by the eye and interpreted in the brain as different colors according to the corresponding wavelengths.

The human eye only perceives wavelengths when lighting is abundant. In low light it looks black and white. In the superposition of light colors (called "additive color synthesis"), the white color results from the sum of all the light colors, while black is the absence of light. In the mixing of secondary light colors (called "subtractive color synthesis"); white only occurs if the pigment or support is of that color, reflecting all the white light, while black is the result of the complete superposition of cyan, magenta and yellow, a mixture that to a certain extent manages to absorb all wavelengths of light.

Primary colors:

Primary color, formerly called primitive color, is considered to be the color that cannot be obtained by mixing any other color. This is an idealized model, based on the biological response of receptor cells in the human eye to the presence of certain frequencies of light and their interferences.

List of primary colors:

  • Red
  • Yellow
  • Blue
  • Mageta
  • White

Secondary colors

Secondary colors are perceptual shades of color, which are obtained by mixing the uneven parts of the primary colors, 2 by 2.

  • Green
  • Violet
  • Orange
